I am a PhD student in the Department of Linguistics at the University of Chicago, where I am a member of the Sociolinguistics Lab (PI: Sharese King) and the Speech Perception & Production Lab (PI: Melissa Baese-Berk). 

My research focuses on the intersubjective nature of identity construction -- how speakers, individual perceivers, and social dynamics and power structures in a given society all play important roles in the connection between linguistic practices and speaker identity. Much of my work focuses on language and gender, particularly with trans communities, and aims at gaining a deeper understanding of how speakers of different genders construct their gender identity in conversations, how racial and gender minorities' speech become "gendered", and the implications and real-world effects of the "gender-ing" process. To do this, I utilize a mixed-methods, interdisciplinary approach; I use experimental and ethnographic methods, quantitative and qualitative analytic frameworks, and incorporate insights from linguistics, anthropology, psychology, queer theory, trans studies, and critical race theory. I also hope to further our understanding of the different cognitive processes that lend themselves to the perception of minoritized voices, including interrogating the roles of experience, stereotype, and gradiency in the perception of phonemes.

Prior to joining UChicago, I completed my B.A. in Linguistics at the University of Michigan and my M.A. in Linguistics at the University of Colorado Boulder, where I was advised by J Calder.
